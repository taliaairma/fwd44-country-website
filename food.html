Food of China

Chinese food isn't just "one thing." It's a whole universe of flavors — and we're breaking it down by four taste sensations: spicy, salty, sweet, and sour. Your mouth is about to travel.

Four Tastes You Need to Try

China's regional cuisines hit every corner of your tongue. Here are four iconic dishes — one for each taste.

SPICY

Mapo Tofu

Region: Szechuan

Soft tofu in a fiery sauce of fermented bean paste, chili oil, and Szechuan peppercorns. The peppercorns create a numbing, tingly sensation — not just heat!

Fun fact: Szechuan peppercorns aren't actually pepper. They're dried berry husks

SALTY

Peking Duck

Region: Beijing

Crispy, shiny skin wrapped in thin pancakes with cucumber, scallions, and sweet bean sauce. The saltiness comes from the traditional air-drying and roasting process.

Fun fact: A master chef slices the duck into exactly 108 pieces.

SWEET

Tanghulu

Region: Northern China (especially Beijing)

Candied hawthorn berries on a stick — like a shiny, crunchy, sweet-and-tiny-sour apple. A classic winter street snack.

Fun fact: Tanghulu dates back to the Song Dynasty (over 800 years ago!).

SOUR

Sour Soup Hotpot

Region: Guizhou

Wild tomatoes and fermented rice water to create a sour-spicy, aromatic, and addictive flavor.

Fun fact: Sour soup is a continuously fermented broth passed down for generations, similar to a sourdough starter.

Flavor Regions of China

China's eight great cuisines each have a personality:

  • Szechuan (Sichuan): Spicy + numbing (see Mapo Tofu above)
  • Cantonese (Guangdong): Mild, fresh, slightly sweet
  • Hunan: Pure spicy (no numbing — just heat!)
  • Jiangsu: Salty + sweet, often braised
  • Zhejiang: Fresh, soft, with a vinegar sourness
